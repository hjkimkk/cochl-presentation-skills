Foundation

Design reviews are moments of alignment

Not just presentations. A well-run review gives everyone the same context, surfaces concerns before they become expensive, and creates a paper trail of decisions.

◈
Shared Context
Everyone understands the problem and constraints before reacting to solutions. No one is surprised by the "why."
◎
Informed Decisions
Decisions are made with research and data, not gut feel. Discovery material earns the audience's trust in the direction.
◇
Faster Iteration
Clear feedback loops reduce rework and ambiguity. Specific questions get specific answers — and unblock the next sprint.

Module 01 · Proposal

What is a Proposal Review?

Purpose Present exploratory work and gather early alignment from stakeholders.
When to run Early in a project phase, before committing to a direction or moving to execution.
Who attends Design lead, Product Manager, and key project stakeholders.
Goal Align on direction and surface concerns early — before they cost time to fix.
Metadata Review Status, Product Stage, Test Coverage, Key Metrics, Next Actions
What this review is NOT
✕
A final decision gate — you're presenting options, not conclusions.
✕
A status report — every slide should tell a story, not list tasks.
✕
A gallery show — context must come before screens, always.

Module 02 · Direction Alignment

What is a Direction Alignment Meeting?

Purpose Align on a single direction before detailed design work begins.
When to run After exploration, when 2–4 refined directions are ready to compare side by side.
Who attends Design lead, PM, Engineering lead, key stakeholders.
Outcome A committed direction with documented rationale and clear next steps.
Required header Phase #, Round #, Team Name, Presenter(s), Date
Why Phase + Round matter

Design reviews happen multiple times in a project. Phase tracks where you are in the product lifecycle. Round tracks how many times you've reviewed this specific feature. Together they give context without a verbal explanation.

Key difference from Proposal

A Proposal asks "which direction should we explore?" A Direction Alignment meeting asks "which refined direction do we commit to?" The stakes and specificity are higher.

Anti-Patterns

Six mistakes that kill design reviews

1
Opening with designs before context. Always lead with goals, constraints, and research. Screens without context invite misguided feedback.
2
No feedback questions defined. Vague asks produce vague responses. Define 3 specific questions before every review — no exceptions.
3
Too many directions presented. 2–3 directions max. More than 3 creates analysis paralysis, not better decisions.
4
Skipping the "why" behind each direction. Always explain why you explored a direction, not just what it looks like. No rationale = no credibility.
5
No clear owner for next steps. Every action needs a name and a date. "The team will look into this" is not an action item.
6
Running a review without research. Discovery data is what separates a proposal from a guess. No competitors, no user data = no credibility.
Quick Reference

Review-day checklist

Before the Review
  • Phase & Round clearly labeled in the header
  • Context set — goals, constraints, and personas defined
  • Discovery data included (market research + user insights)
  • Design process timeline visible with current stage marked
  • Feedback questions pre-defined (max 3 specific questions)
  • All directions named, distinct, and individually justified
  • Decision tool selected and prepared (matrix, comparison, poll)
  • Figma prototype or final mockup linked
During the Review
  • Don't pitch — facilitate. Let the material do the work.
  • Guide the audience through your 3 feedback questions
  • Capture decisions and rationale in real time
  • If discussion drifts, redirect to the feedback questions
  • Call out when a decision is being made — name it explicitly
  • Agree on next steps before closing the meeting
  • Every action has a name and a date before you close
  • Share the deck + decision record within 24 hours
